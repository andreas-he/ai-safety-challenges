# Elicitation vs. Capability — Does the Supervisor's Ceiling Matter?

**Date:** 2026-04-28 | **Format:** Multiple-choice | **Category:** Evaluation & alignment | **Difficulty:** Quick (5–15 min)

**Streak:** Day 12

---

## Relevance

The elicitation/capability gap is a central concern for capability evaluations and alignment research: a model may harbour dangerous capabilities that safety evals fail to surface, not because the model lacks them, but because our elicitation method doesn't activate them. Understanding why weak supervisors can still unlock strong-model performance (Burns et al. 2023) is direct preparation for evaluating model dangerousness in realistic settings.

---

## Setup

From Burns et al. (2023), *Weak-to-Strong Generalization* (OpenAI):

> We finetune large pretrained models using labels generated by weaker models. We call this *weak-to-strong generalization*. In our core experiment, a GPT-2-level **weak supervisor** generates binary labels for a GPT-4-class **strong student**. We measure **Performance Gap Recovered (PGR)**: 0 means the student matches the weak supervisor; 1 means the student matches a human-labeled ground-truth ceiling.
>
> On most NLP benchmarks, including tasks hard enough that the weak supervisor labels incorrectly >30% of the time, we find PGR significantly above zero — the strong student outperforms its own supervisor.

## Question

Which explanation **best** accounts for PGR > 0 on hard tasks where the weak supervisor's labels are frequently wrong?

**(A)** The strong student learns a meta-strategy for detecting supervisor errors and systematically inverts low-confidence labels.

**(B)** The student's pre-training already encodes the task capability; even imperfect supervision provides enough directional signal to *elicit* that latent knowledge — no new knowledge is taught.

**(C)** PGR > 0 is a statistical artifact: the weak supervisor's errors are random, so on average the noisy labels still point in the correct direction for most examples.

**(D)** The student generalises from the easy subset of examples where the supervisor is correct, then applies a learned rule to harder out-of-distribution examples, effectively extrapolating beyond the supervisor's knowledge.

**(E)** The strong student is pre-trained to be sycophantic toward supervisors; when the supervisor happens to be correct on hard examples, the student disproportionately weights those signals.

---

<details>
<summary><b>Solution</b></summary>

**Correct answer: B**

---

**B is correct.** The W2SG paper's central claim is that strong models already encode most task capabilities during pre-training on vast corpora. Weak supervision does not *teach* the student new knowledge — it *elicits* (activates) knowledge the student already has. Even noisy positive signal is sufficient to unlock a latent ability, which is why PGR > 0 even when the supervisor is wrong on a substantial fraction of examples. The paper introduces the term **elicitation failure** to distinguish this from genuine **capability failure**.

**A is wrong.** The paper does not find evidence that students learn an error-detection meta-strategy. The student treats supervisor labels as positive signal, not as something to invert.

**C is wrong.** If labels were uniformly random, expected training signal would be uninformative noise — PGR would cluster near 0, not significantly above it. The distribution of supervisor errors is correlated (supervisors fail systematically on *hard* patterns, not randomly), so this doesn't apply.

**D is partially tempting** but mischaracterises the mechanism. "Extrapolation from easy examples" implies the student learns a *new generalisation rule* from supervision. The actual mechanism is that the capability was always in the model; the easy-to-hard split reflects whether the supervisor's labels are strong enough to *activate* a pre-existing representation.

**E is wrong.** Sycophancy is about following *user preference signals at inference time*, not about up-weighting supervisor agreement during training. The framing also has causality backwards.

---

**Key insight for safety:** The elicitation/capability gap has direct safety implications. A model may have dangerous capabilities that *current* safety evaluations fail to surface — not because the capability is absent, but because the eval methodology doesn't elicit it. Robust capability evaluation therefore requires adversarial elicitation (varied prompting, fine-tuning probes) in addition to standard benchmarks. This is the practical motivation behind the W2SG research programme.
